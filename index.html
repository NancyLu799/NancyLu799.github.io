About me

I am a junior at Yale University completing a double major in biology and stats. I am particularly interested in computational biology, especially genomics, as well as biostatistics. Currently, my research in the Pyle Lab focuses on using computational techniques to analyze covariate base pairs in RNA structure. This covariation across distantly related species can tell us about the evolutionary history of RNA, which differs considerably from that of DNA. Ultimately, with this knowledge, we aim to expand software that gives 3D predictions of various RNA tertiary structures from their nucleic acid sequences. My advisors are Dr. Moreno and Dr. Pyle.

The fusion of mathematics and biology fascinates me. After competing extensively in national high school math competitions including USAMO, USAMTS, and Math Prize, I knew that I loved the thrill of piecing together mathematical puzzles. At Yale, I fell in love with biology, and subsequently have found the fusion of mathematical techniques and biology to be especially interesting.

Research Papers and Publications

Model to Predict Alzheimer's Disease

This is my final project for CBB 452: Biomedical Data Science (Mining and Modeling). The goal of this project was to calculate the Alzheimer's Disease risk of a patient, given his genome. Given the high heritability of Alzheimer's Disease as well as its late onset, studying this disease has immense potential in genomics. We used SNP annotation tools alongside GWAS studies in order to acccomplish this task.

Analyzing Effects of Mental Health in Bariatric Surgery Patients

Since the summer of 2016, I have been working at the Magee-Womens Research Center (University of Pittsburgh Medical Center) on analyzing outcomes of bariatric surgery, and how these outcomes relate to mental health factors. Through statistical analyses including repeated-measured ANCOVA analyses and interaction analysis, I studied a large cohort of bariatric surgery outcomes and found that former physical, mental, or sexual abuse played a significant role in surgical outcomes. Paper to be published in late 2018.

Creating Validated Mental Health Scales for Children

To help answer the question when children should seek help for mental health issues, I helped create 2 validated psychological scales through principle components analysis of results from extensive focus groups and prior research. These scales are targeted to parents and guardians to determine the best time to seek treatment for children's mental health. This research was presented at the American Psychological Society's annual conference (May 2017).

Engineering Plastic-Digesting Bacteria

I was part of Yale's iGEM (international Genomically Engineered Machinery) team in the summer of 2017. Our goal was to genetically E. coli capable of digesting polyethylene terephthalate (PET) plastics efficiently in a landfill situation. To do this, multiple non-native pathways including a PETase and MHETase pathway were engineered into the bacteria. The bacteria then underwent adaptive evolution and multiplex autonomic genomic engineering (MAGE) to increase the rate of plastic metabolism. We will compete later in the International Jamboree this year (Summer 2017).

Other Presentations and Papers

Analyzing LGBT Rights in Israel

For my final project in SOCY 155: State and Society of Israel, I examined the state of LGBT rights in Israel. I was particularly interested in the interaction between the publicly Jewish society and the many progressive political changes in Israel. In particular, are Israel's outwardly progressive LGBT stances truly representative of its people? This paper received third place for the Yale Acheson Prize in International Relations. (June 2018)

Mitigating Effects of the Opioid Epidemic

In this piece, I examine the opioid epidemic in the Yale Global Health Review. Having known people who have passed away from opioid overdose, it is clear that this issue has become one of the most prevalent and pressing public health problems in the US. In this article, I provide an overview of the current challenges and proposed solutions. I have been involved with YGHR in various capacities since fall 2016, including serving as its Editor-in-chief in 2017-2018.

Yale-China Internship

During this Yale-China internship, I volunteered at New Life Psychiatric Rehabilitation Center, the largest psychiatric facility in Hong Kong. Based on an innovative place-train-place model which gives those with severe psychiatric illnesses the ability to work (with guidance) in New Life businesses, this non-profit has been highly lauded as an example for many other countries. At the conclusion of this internship, I presented about the differences in the mental health systems of HK and the US (summer 2017).

Improving Crop Yields through Genetic Engineering

One of the most pressing agricultural problems in the world is that many plant species have reached yield plateaus due to climate change. In this paper, I examine how we can use genetic engineering to insert Wuschel (WUS) and baby boom (BBM) genes as morphogenic regulators to turn sorghum cells totipotent, and thus increase their flowering time and crop yield. I used eQTL analysis and traditional genetic methods. This was the final project for my MCDB 201: Molecular Biology class (spring 2017).
